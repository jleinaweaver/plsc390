Affective Polarization


“Democrats and Republicans both say that the other party’s members are hypocritical, selfish, and closed-minded, and they are unwilling to socialize across party lines, or even to partner with opponents in a variety of other activities. This phenomenon of animosity between the parties is known as affective polarization” (130).

  • How is this different from the issue polarization we studied last week?


This is a separate divide from policy polarization.

  • “Rather, we argue that affective polarization is largely distinct from the ideological divide, and that extremity in issue opinions is not a necessary condition for affective polarization (Iyengar et al. 2012, Mason 2015).”

  • Indeed, in some settings, affective polarization can increase while ideological divisions shrink (Levendusky & Malhotra 2016a)” (131).

  3. Behavioral Measures (133-134)

The authors discuss these three approaches to measuring affective polarization in the mass public.

NOTES

How do we measure affective polarization? (131-134) Three main techniques…

Technique 1. Survey Self-Reports (131-132)

  • Approach 1: ANES feeling thermometer approach, respondents asked to rate each party on a scale from cold (0) to warm (100). “Affective polarization is then computed as the difference between the score given to the party of the respondent and the score given to the opposing party, which we also refer to as the out party” (131).

    • Figure 1 (132) shows: a significant increase in affective polarization since 1980, the change is not that people like their own party more over time; rather, there is an increase in out-party animus, especially in recent years, and affective polarization actually decreased between 2012 and 2016

  • Approach 2: “use trait ratings of party supporters to measure affective discord: Are they intelligent, open-minded, and generous, or hypocritical, selfish, and mean trait ratings” (131) OR ask people to list what they like and dislike about each party OR measure positive/negative reactions to presidential candidates

  • Approach 3: “A less obtrusive measure of partisan affect is social distance, the extent to which individuals feel comfortable interacting with out-group members in various settings. If partisanship is an important social identity in its own right, partisans should be averse to entering into close interpersonal relations with their opponents” (132).


Technique 2. Implicit Measures (133)

  • Since survey respondents may misrepresent their positions due to normative pressures, “Iyengar & Westwood (2015) developed an Implicit Association Test to document unconscious partisan bias, based on the brief version of the race IAT.”


Technique 3. Behavioral Measures (133-134)

  • Since the implicit measures “are weak predictors of relevant behaviors,” “scholars have turned to behavioral manifestations of partisan animus in both lab and naturalistic settings.”

  • Economic games like the trust game and the dictator game

  • Other experiments in nonpolitical settings, e.g. review job candidates that are identical except for experimentally varied race, ethnicity, gender or partisanship.

  • Much evidence of partisanship mattering in these experiments

NOTES

  1. Lit: Partisan sorting leads to AP

    • “Sorting likely leads people to perceive both opposing partisans and copartisans as more extreme than they really are, with misperceptions about opposing partisans being more acute (Levendusky & Malhotra 2016b).”

    • Mason (2015 and 2018) “has shown that those with consistent partisan and ideological identities became more hostile toward the out party without necessarily changing their ideological positions, and those who have aligned religious, racial, and partisan identities react more emotionally to information that threatens their partisan identities or issue stances” (134).

  2. Lit: Ideological polarization (e.g. issue polarization) increases AP

  3. Lit: Growing availability of partisan news sources may increase AP (precise mechanism unclear); This dynamic may predominantly only impact those already holding the most extreme positions.

  4. Lit: Debate remains about the effect of social media on AP

  5. Other possible mechanisms: Mainstream media coverage of polarization has been shown to increase AP, political campaigns can increase AP and the existence of echo chambers (if they are real) may increase AP

NOTES

Decreasing Affective Polarization (139-141)

  • “First, scholars have shown that correcting misperceptions about party supporters reduces animus toward the other side (Ahler & Sood 2018).”

  • “A second approach tries to shift the salience of respondents’ partisan identities. … Using a set of survey experiments, as well as a natural experiment stemming from the July Fourth holiday, Levendusky (2018a) shows that emphasizing American identity reduces animus toward the other party.”

  • “More generally, the evidence suggests that making partisanship and politics less salient—and emphasizing other factors—can potentially change behavior as well. … In an era of affective polarization, downplaying politics can help to mitigate partisan divisions.”

  • BUT! 1) “First, while both types of efforts appear to be effective, we should not expect reduction of partisan animus to be easy, even in the survey context, where behavior tends to be quite malleable. While some strategies will work, many sensible strategies will fail.” 2) “Further, it is unclear to what extent treatments that work in a survey experiment (or other controlled settings) work in the messy environment of real-world politics.”

  • “One potentially promising strand of research is to build on the insights of intergroup contact theory (Pettigrew & Tropp 2011) and examine whether constructive engagement between Democrats and Republicans could potentially reduce partisan animus.”
